## 1. Commerce closes AI chip export loophole targeting Chinese entities abroad
**U.S. Bureau of Industry and Security · May 31, 2026 · Yesterday**

The BIS posted weekend guidance mandating that Nvidia's Blackwell and Rubin chip families and AMD's MI350x require export licenses when sold to any entity headquartered in China, regardless of where that entity is physically located. The rule closes a gap that opened in May 2025 when Commerce declined to enforce the Biden-era AI Diffusion rule; industry sources estimate "hundreds of thousands" of advanced chips reached Chinese buyers through subsidiaries in Malaysia and other third countries during that window. The guidance does not require removal of already-deployed hardware.

*Why it matters:* Closing the offshore-entity loophole tightens the AI compute chokepoint just as Nvidia's Rubin generation enters volume production, escalating the U.S.–China semiconductor arms race at the worst possible moment for buyers who relied on the gap.

## 2. Google Gemini 3.5 Flash beats last-gen Pro, reshapes search and agentic stack
**Google · May 19–20, 2026 · 12 days ago · Still developing**

Google shipped Gemini 3.5 Flash immediately at I/O 2026, with the model scoring 78% on SWE-bench Verified, 84.2% on CharXiv multimodal, and 83.6% on MCP Atlas agentic benchmark—surpassing Gemini 3.1 Pro on "nearly all" evals. The model runs at 280+ output tokens per second with a 1M-token context window, priced at $1.50/$9.00 per million input/output tokens with a 90% cache discount. Google simultaneously unveiled a redesigned search box accepting text, images, PDFs, video, and Chrome tabs, all powered by 3.5 Flash. Gemini 3.5 Pro, in internal use now, is expected to roll out publicly in June.

*Why it matters:* Flash-tier clearing prior-generation Pro benchmarks at half the cost compresses the price-performance curve for every developer building on Google's stack and sets the competitive bar ahead of expected Anthropic and OpenAI releases.

## 3. AMD EPYC Venice enters production on TSMC 2nm — first HPC chip at the node
**AMD · May 21, 2026 · 11 days ago**

AMD announced the production ramp of its 6th-generation EPYC "Venice" server CPU on TSMC's N2 (2nm-class) process — the first high-performance compute chip in the industry to reach this node. Wafer production is currently expanding in Taiwan, with TSMC's Arizona fab as the next site. Venice slots into AMD's Helios rack-scale platform alongside the Instinct MI450X GPU, targeting multi-gigawatt hyperscale deployments in H2 2026. A follow-on 2nm product, codenamed Verano, is already on AMD's public roadmap.

*Why it matters:* The jump to 2nm gives AMD a measurable node lead over Nvidia Blackwell (TSMC 4nm), tightening competitive pressure in the CPU-plus-accelerator platform race that hyperscalers are buying at multi-gigawatt scale.

## 4. IonQ–SkyWater merger clears shareholder vote; quantum vertical integration nears close
**IonQ / SkyWater Technology · May 9, 2026 · 23 days ago · Still developing**

SkyWater Technology shareholders approved IonQ's $1.8 billion acquisition (cash-and-stock at $35/share), first announced in January 2026. The deal awaits regulatory sign-off and is expected to close in Q2–Q3 2026. Combined, IonQ and SkyWater would form the only vertically integrated full-stack quantum company with embedded access to a trusted U.S. semiconductor foundry. IonQ's roadmap targets 200,000-qubit QPUs beginning functional testing at SkyWater's fabs in 2028, enabling 8,000 ultra-high-fidelity logical qubits.

*Why it matters:* Owning the manufacturing stack removes the exotic-materials foundry dependency that every other major quantum company currently outsources, giving IonQ a structural advantage as the industry moves toward fault-tolerant systems.

## 5. Thea Energy closes $100M stellarator Series B to build Eos machine
**Thea Energy · May 27, 2026 · 5 days ago**

Princeton spinout Thea Energy closed an oversubscribed $100M Series B led by Thomas Tull's US Innovative Technology Fund. Proceeds will expand planar-coil magnet manufacturing, open a second New Jersey facility, and fund site selection and groundbreaking for the Eos integrated stellarator. The close coincided with the NRC's 90-day public comment period on its new fusion regulatory framework ending May 27, putting licensing rules on track for October 2026 finalization.

*Why it matters:* Stellarators sidestep the plasma disruption problems that have long plagued tokamak designs; Thea's funding surge—arriving as the U.S. regulatory pathway solidifies—signals the concept is transitioning from decades of research to credible near-term engineering.

## Under the radar

**Anthropic in early talks to run Claude on Microsoft Maia 200 · May 21–24, 2026**
Anthropic is negotiating to lease Azure capacity running Microsoft's Maia 200 AI accelerator — a second-generation custom inference chip launched in January 2026 on TSMC 3nm, claiming >30% better tokens-per-dollar than Nvidia silicon in Microsoft's fleet. No deal has closed; Maia 200 remains in limited preview. If signed, it would be the first public validation of Maia 200 by an external frontier lab. Anthropic already runs Claude across Nvidia GPUs plus Amazon Trainium, Google TPUs, and its own ASIC program — Maia would be a fourth custom-silicon lane.

*Why it matters:* Frontier labs actively shopping inference capacity across competing custom accelerators is the clearest sign yet that Nvidia's lock on training-era dominance does not automatically extend to the inference era.
