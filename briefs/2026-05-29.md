## 1. Altman and Amodei Both Reverse AI Jobs Apocalypse Predictions
**OpenAI / Anthropic · May 26–27 · 2–3 days ago**

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman told Commonwealth Bank of Australia CEO Matt Comyn he was "pretty wrong" about AI's economic impact, saying the entry-level job displacement he warned about in mid-2025 simply hasn't materialized. He described personally testing AI-delegated Slack and email, then reverting to handling it himself. Dario Amodei, who once warned AI could eliminate 50% of white-collar jobs within two to three years, reframed automation this month as a "multiplier of output" rather than a net destroyer of employment.

*Why it matters:* Both companies are preparing trillion-dollar IPOs, and critics note the timing is not coincidental — a softened narrative plays better to institutional investors than a jobs apocalypse pitch.

## 2. Illinois Passes Frontier AI Safety Audit Bill
**Illinois General Assembly · May 28 · 1 day ago**

Illinois lawmakers passed SB 315, requiring frontier AI companies — explicitly naming OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google DeepMind — to submit to mandatory third-party safety audits. If Governor Pritzker signs it, the bill becomes one of the strongest state-level AI oversight laws in the U.S., filling the regulatory vacuum left by the Trump administration's no-new-federal-rulemaking stance announced in March.

*Why it matters:* With no federal AI safety framework in sight, state laws are becoming the de facto oversight layer; SB 315 could set the audit-requirement template that other large states follow.

## 3. Nvidia H20 Export Rules: $4.5B Q1 Charge, $8B Q2 Revenue Hit Projected
**Nvidia · May 28 · 1 day ago**

Nvidia disclosed that U.S. licensing requirements on its H20 chips — the export-controlled version of its AI accelerator built for China — cost $4.5 billion in Q1 charges. The company projects an $8 billion revenue hit in Q2, the largest single-quarter financial consequence yet from the U.S.-China semiconductor war.

*Why it matters:* The scale of these losses confirms that export controls are now a first-order business constraint for Nvidia, will accelerate Chinese domestic chip alternatives, and will pressure U.S. allies to decide which side of the semiconductor divide they sit on.

## 4. SPARC Fusion Reactor Reaches 75% Completion in Massachusetts
**Commonwealth Fusion Systems · May 2026 · Still developing**

Engineers at Commonwealth Fusion Systems lowered the second half of SPARC's vacuum vessel by crane at their Devens, MA facility, bringing the tokamak to 75% complete. The reactor remains on schedule for first plasma in 2026, with a net energy demonstration targeting fusion gain Q > 2 in 2027. CFS is using AI-assisted design tools from Siemens and Nvidia to compress the build timeline.

*Why it matters:* SPARC is the leading commercial bet on high-temperature superconducting tokamaks; hitting the 2026 first-plasma window keeps the 2030s commercial fusion timeline credible.

## 5. AMD Acquires Enosemi to Enter Silicon Photonics
**AMD · May 28 · 1 day ago**

AMD acquired Enosemi, a silicon photonics startup whose technology uses light photons rather than copper traces to transmit data at scale. The deal gives AMD ownership of a key layer in AI data center infrastructure: high-bandwidth, low-power interconnects between accelerators, CPUs, and memory.

*Why it matters:* As AI cluster scale pushes electrical interconnects to their physical limits, silicon photonics is increasingly the chokepoint — AMD is acquiring the capability rather than remaining dependent on third-party suppliers like Broadcom's optical divisions.

## Under the radar

**Colorado rewrites its landmark AI Act (SB 26-189) · May 9 · Still developing**  
Colorado replaced SB 24-205 with a narrower law focused on "automated decision-making technology" rather than the original broad "high-risk AI systems" frame, delaying implementation to January 1, 2027. The rewrite substantially reduces employer obligations and requires mandatory rulemaking before it takes effect.

*Why it matters:* Every state watching Colorado's first-mover experiment just saw the scope shrink under business pressure — a cautionary data point for any legislator drafting ambitious AI regulation.

**ByteDance developing custom CPUs to cut foreign chip dependence · May 28 · 1 day ago**  
ByteDance is reportedly building its own CPU chips — a step beyond its existing custom AI accelerators — to reduce reliance on foreign suppliers as U.S. export controls remain in force.

*Why it matters:* Full CPU self-sufficiency would give ByteDance infrastructure independence at the deepest hardware layer and signals that leading Chinese tech firms are pursuing end-to-end semiconductor decoupling in earnest.
