## 1. Anthropic Locks Down Claude Mythos After It Finds Thousands of Zero-Days
**Anthropic · April 2026 · Still developing**

Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview, deployed internally under Project Glasswing, identified thousands of previously unknown vulnerabilities across every major operating system and web browser within weeks of testing — including critical flaws that had evaded human security researchers. Because of its demonstrated capacity to operate at offensive-security scale, Anthropic declined public release and instead granted access to over 40 organizations — including AWS, Apple, Cisco, Google, JPMorgan Chase, Linux Foundation, Microsoft, and Nvidia — for coordinated patching. It is the first time a frontier model has been deliberately withheld on security grounds with this level of industry-wide coordination.

*Why it matters:* Claude Mythos sets a new baseline for what AI can do in offensive security, and the Project Glasswing response establishes a precedent for how labs handle models deemed too dangerous to open-source.

## 2. Four Chinese Open-Weights Coding Models Drop in 12 Days
**Z.ai / MiniMax / Moonshot AI / DeepSeek · Early May 2026 · Still developing**

GLM-5.1, MiniMax M2.7, Kimi K2.6, and DeepSeek V4 all shipped within a 12-day window in early May, each converging at near-frontier coding and agentic engineering performance. DeepSeek V4 Pro — 1.6 trillion parameters, MIT-licensed — benchmarks near the top of open-weight leaderboards and costs $3.48 per million output tokens; OpenAI charges $30 for equivalent throughput. None of the four models costs more than a third of Claude Opus 4.7 at inference.

*Why it matters:* China's coordinated, low-cost open-weights push is compressing the global pricing floor for frontier coding capability, making Western labs' premium tiers increasingly hard to justify for enterprise buyers shopping on cost.

## 3. Google and SpaceX in Active Talks to Launch Orbital AI Data Centers
**Google / SpaceX · May 12–15, 2026 · 1–4 days ago**

Per a Wall Street Journal report, Google is in active discussions with SpaceX to launch its first orbital data center test payload, timed partly to SpaceX's planned $1.75 trillion IPO later this year. The talks connect to Google's Project Suncatcher, revealed last November, which targets prototype satellites loaded with Tensor Processing Units by 2027. SpaceX is pitching orbital infrastructure as the cheapest long-run option for AI compute; today, once satellite construction and launch costs are included, terrestrial data centers remain significantly cheaper.

*Why it matters:* Orbital compute, if viable, would bypass the two biggest constraints on AI infrastructure growth — power grid access and local zoning opposition — while positioning SpaceX as a strategic backbone for hyperscale AI.

## 4. Samsung Chip Factory Strike Countdown: Talks Collapsed, Walkout Set May 21
**Samsung Electronics · May 12–15, 2026 · Yesterday**

Negotiations between Samsung's largest union — now 73,000 members — and management broke down over bonus caps tied to AI-driven memory chip profits. The union is demanding 15% of operating profit allocated to worker bonuses with no cap; management has not agreed. Samsung executives made a rare direct visit to union leadership Friday in a last-ditch effort, but the union confirmed the 18-day strike will proceed May 21. Analysts project 30,000–40,000 workers will walk out, with losses of approximately 1 trillion won (~$700 million) per day from factory shutdowns.

*Why it matters:* A sustained walkout at Samsung would directly hit HBM and DRAM production at a moment when AI accelerator demand is outrunning supply — and SK Hynix alone cannot absorb the gap.

## 5. NASA's New Space Processor Tests at 500x Current Chip Performance
**NASA / JPL / Microchip Technology · May 13–15, 2026 · 1–3 days ago**

NASA's High Performance Spaceflight Computing (HPSC) processor — developed by Microchip Technology under a JPL commercial partnership — is benchmarking at 500 times the throughput of the radiation-hardened chips currently flying in space. Small enough to fit in a palm, the processor is designed to run AI inference onboard, enabling spacecraft to make autonomous real-time decisions without waiting for Earth-based commands. Once certified, NASA plans to integrate it across Earth orbiters, planetary rovers, crewed habitats, and deep-space missions.

*Why it matters:* Onboard AI inference at this performance level is a prerequisite for serious autonomy on Mars missions, where the one-way communication delay to Earth averages 13 minutes and makes real-time ground guidance impossible.

## Under the radar

**Gallup: 71% of Americans oppose local AI data centers · May 2026**

A new Gallup survey found nearly three in four U.S. adults oppose siting an AI data center in their community, with opposition cutting across party lines. The data arrives as hyperscalers push to fast-track permitting for hundreds of new facilities across Virginia, Texas, and the Midwest, often against local resistance over water use, noise, and grid load.

*Why it matters:* Public opposition at this scale creates a structural constraint on domestic AI compute expansion that neither federal permitting reform nor tax incentives can easily override, and it will increasingly shape where new capacity can actually be built.
