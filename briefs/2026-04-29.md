*The US–China AI divide hardened this week: Beijing unwound a major American acquisition while Washington's tech firms raced to sign defense deals their own employees oppose.*

## 1. China Vetoes Meta's $2B Manus Acquisition
**Meta / China MOFCOM · April 27, 2026 · 2 days ago**

China's Ministry of Commerce ordered Meta and Manus to unwind their $2 billion deal, citing national security and technology-export concerns. The decision came after Beijing banned two Manus co-founders from leaving the country during a months-long investigation. The complication: Meta had already integrated Manus into its internal systems and absorbed its executive team after closing the deal in December 2025. Meta said the transaction "complied fully with applicable law" and called for "an appropriate resolution."

*Why it matters:* Beijing's willingness to block and reverse a deal post-integration signals that Chinese regulatory power now extends to mid-execution M&A—a direct warning to any US firm pursuing AI talent with Chinese roots.

## 2. Google Signs Classified AI Deal with Pentagon
**Google / U.S. DoD · April 28, 2026 · 1 day ago**

Google granted the Department of Defense access to its AI systems for "any lawful government purpose," signing a classified agreement that covers sensitive military contexts. The contract was finalized after Anthropic declined to offer comparable terms—specifically over autonomous weapons and domestic mass-surveillance provisions. Over 950 Google employees have signed an open letter urging leadership to reverse course. OpenAI and xAI signed similar DoD agreements earlier this year.

*Why it matters:* Anthropic's refusal and the internal revolt expose a genuine fracture in the industry over military use guardrails, and Google's signature now sets the de-facto commercial baseline.

## 3. DeepSeek V4 Pro and Flash Released · Still developing
**DeepSeek · April 24, 2026 · 5 days ago**

DeepSeek released two open-weights models: V4-Pro (1.6 trillion parameters, Mixture-of-Experts) and V4-Flash (284 billion parameters for cost-sensitive workloads). V4-Pro scores 80.6% on SWE-bench Verified—within 0.2 points of Claude Opus 4.6—and leads all open models on math, STEM, and agentic coding. Both ship with a 1-million-token default context window; a hybrid attention mechanism cuts single-token inference FLOPs to 27% of V3's.

*Why it matters:* An open-weights model this close to closed-frontier performance raises the floor for every enterprise weighing whether to pay for proprietary API access.

## 4. OpenAI Ships GPT-5.5 · Still developing
**OpenAI · April 23, 2026 · 6 days ago**

OpenAI released GPT-5.5 (codename: Spud) to Plus, Pro, Business, and Enterprise tiers in ChatGPT and Codex; API access opened April 24. The model matches GPT-5.4's per-token latency while operating at higher intelligence and is described as more token-efficient despite a higher list price. Performance gains concentrate in agentic coding, computer use, and long-horizon research—the same categories DeepSeek V4 is targeting.

*Why it matters:* GPT-5.5's agentic focus, arriving the same week as V4, intensifies a race where "doing" is replacing "answering" as the core product value proposition.

## 5. Chinese Humanoid Robot Runs Half-Marathon in 50:26 · Still developing
**Honor / Beijing E-Town · April 19, 2026 · 10 days ago**

Honor's bipedal robot "Lightning" completed the 21 km Beijing E-Town course in 50 minutes, 26 seconds—over 6 minutes under the human world record (56:42). More than 100 teams entered this year's race, up from roughly 20 last year; the top three finishers ran fully autonomously. Lightning runs on a liquid-cooled system with 95 cm legs modeled on elite sprint athletes. The 2025 event's winner finished in 2 hours, 40 minutes.

*Why it matters:* A two-hour-plus improvement in 12 months, across dozens of competing teams, is a compounding capability signal—not a publicity stunt.

EU negotiators held the final political trilogue on the Digital Omnibus on AI, voting on hard implementation-delay dates that could push several AI Act obligations out by 12–24 months. The outcome directly affects how quickly EU-based companies must comply with high-risk AI system requirements.

*Why it matters:* A delay gives European firms operational breathing room but weakens the EU's window to shape global AI governance norms before the US framework solidifies.

**Fusion's Cost Problem — April 2026**

The finding lands as ARPA-E commits $135 million to fusion R&D and multiple startups pursue commercial plant siting.

*Why it matters:* If the cost-curve analysis holds, venture-backed fusion timelines built on optimistic learning-rate assumptions are materially exposed.
