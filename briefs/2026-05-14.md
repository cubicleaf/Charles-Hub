*Trump's first Beijing summit turned AI chip policy and tech CEO dealmaking into the week's defining geopolitical flashpoint.*

## 1. Trump Lands in Beijing with Huang, Cook, and Musk; H200 Exports Conditionally Cleared
**Trump/Commerce Dept · May 13–14 · Today**

President Trump arrived in Beijing on May 13 accompanied by Nvidia's Jensen Huang (added at the last minute after Trump called him personally, overriding initial exclusion by national-security hawks), Apple's Tim Cook, Tesla's Elon Musk, and executives from Qualcomm, Micron, Meta, Goldman Sachs, and others. Simultaneously, the Commerce Department cleared H200 chip sales to roughly 10 Chinese firms—including Alibaba, Tencent, ByteDance, and JD.com—conditional on guaranteed U.S. supply adequacy, third-party security testing in the U.S., and buyer export compliance programs. No deliveries have been made; Chinese firms reportedly pulled back pending guidance from Beijing. Summit agenda: rare earths, AI chip access, Taiwan, and Iran.

*Why it matters:* AI chips are now the primary currency in U.S.-China negotiations, and Huang's late addition signals the administration is betting commercial engagement over technology denial.

## 2. Anthropic's Claude Mythos Autonomously Finds Thousands of Zero-Days Across Every Major OS
**Anthropic · Late April–May 11 · Still developing**

Anthropic announced Project Glasswing and gave roughly 44 organizations—Apple, Google, Microsoft, AWS, Nvidia, JPMorganChase, the Linux Foundation, CrowdStrike, Cisco, and others—limited access to Claude Mythos Preview. Over several weeks, Mythos autonomously found thousands of previously unknown critical vulnerabilities in every major operating system and browser. One disclosed example: it identified and exploited CVE-2026-4747, a 17-year-old remote code execution flaw in FreeBSD's NFS stack that grants unauthenticated root access from anywhere on the internet. Over 99% of the findings remain unpatched; coordinated disclosure is ongoing. Mythos Preview is not yet publicly available.

*Why it matters:* Autonomous AI-driven vulnerability discovery at this scale permanently compresses the gap between capability emergence and exploitation—defenders without equivalent tools are already behind.

## 3. Google and SpaceX in Advanced Talks on Orbital AI Data Centers
**Google/SpaceX · May 12 · 2 days ago**

The Wall Street Journal reported Google is in advanced negotiations with SpaceX for rocket-launch services to place AI data centers in orbit. Google's internal effort, Project Suncatcher, envisions 81 satellites spanning a 1 km radius forming a compute cluster loaded with Google's Tensor Processing Units; prototype launches target 2027. SpaceX is using the talks as a pillar of its pre-IPO narrative—positioning orbital compute as eventually cheaper than terrestrial alternatives—ahead of its planned $1.75 trillion IPO later in 2026. Separately, Anthropic also signed a deal last week to use SpaceX compute capacity, with an option for future orbital collaboration.

*Why it matters:* Orbital compute would sidestep the land, water, and grid-power bottlenecks now binding the pace of AI infrastructure expansion.

## 4. Gemini Becomes Android's Intelligence Layer; Google Enters AI PC Market
**Google · May 12 · 2 days ago · Still developing — I/O keynote May 19**

Google's "The Android Show" pre-event on May 12 revealed that Gemini Intelligence is moving below the app layer to become the embedded AI substrate of Android itself. Google also announced Googlebook—a line of Gemini-native laptops from Acer, ASUS, Dell, HP, and Lenovo, launching fall 2026—and confirmed Android 17 will ship floating "Bubble" app windows. The full Google I/O 2026 keynote is May 19 at 10 AM PT in Mountain View.

*Why it matters:* In a single announcement cycle Google is vertically integrating AI into the OS and hardware layers simultaneously, directly challenging Microsoft's Copilot PC strategy ahead of the holiday season.

## 5. IonQ Posts Record $64.7M Q1 Revenue, +755% YoY; Sells First 256-Qubit System
**IonQ · May 6 · 8 days ago · Still developing**

IonQ reported Q1 2026 revenue of $64.7 million, beating its guidance midpoint by ~30% and growing 755% year-over-year. Remaining performance obligations grew 554% YoY to $470 million. The company sold its first 6th-generation, chip-fabricated 256-qubit trapped-ion system to the University of Cambridge and raised its 2026 full-year revenue guidance to $260–270 million, targeting over 100% organic growth.

*Why it matters:* Sustained triple-digit revenue growth from actual system sales—not just contracts—signals quantum computing is crossing from research procurement to enterprise deployment.
