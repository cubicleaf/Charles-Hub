Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz on 20 June 2026, one day after Israel and Hezbollah agreed to a ceasefire mediated by Qatar and the United States, citing Israel's continued strikes across southern Lebanon as a breach of the US-Iran Memorandum of Understanding.

"It is hereby announced that the Strait of Hormuz will be closed to vessel traffic," the Khatam-al Anbiya Central Headquarters announced in a statement carried by Iran's state Mehr news agency. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps confirmed the closure and warned vessels not to approach. Iran characterized the closure as a first escalatory step: "It is noted that this first step is a response to the enemy's breach of promise, and if the aggression continues, further steps will be planned and taken to force the enemy to comply with its obligations."

The IRGC cited both Israeli attacks on Lebanon and US violations of the ceasefire as grounds for the closure.

The reclosure followed a day in which Israeli forces killed at least 29 people across southern Lebanon. Strikes on Nabatieh killed 16 and wounded 12, according to Lebanon's civil defence. An Israeli strike on the village of Barish in Tyre killed four members of the same family, the Lebanese National News Agency reported. Seven people were killed and 13 injured in a strike near Sidon. A Lebanese army officer was killed in an Israeli air strike on the Kfar Rumman-Nabatieh road; the Lebanese army said in a statement that "the continued brutal Israeli attacks aim to obstruct any solution that would allow for the restoration of stability in Lebanon."

The ceasefire between Israel and Hezbollah had been announced by Trump on Friday afternoon — less than 24 hours before the renewed Israeli offensive and the subsequent Hormuz closure. Two days earlier, CENTCOM had lifted the US naval blockade on Iranian ports as part of the MoU's implementation.

A fifth of the world's crude oil and liquefied natural gas passes through the strait. Iran had effectively closed it for nearly four months following the onset of the US-Israel war in late February 2026. Its reclosure — triggered by Israeli conduct that Tehran characterizes as a breach of the MoU — directly imperils both the agreement's survival and global energy markets.

Iran's foreign ministry said its negotiating delegation would travel to Switzerland to "follow up and demand implementation of the other party's commitments" under the MoU. Switzerland confirmed that follow-up implementation talks at Burgenstock would proceed, though the Iranian delegation's departure was reportedly delayed by the escalated Israeli attacks.

The reclosure creates a direct test of the MoU's durability: if the US does not pressure Israel to halt its Lebanon offensive, Iran has signaled it will not honor the strait's reopening or the agreement's other terms.
