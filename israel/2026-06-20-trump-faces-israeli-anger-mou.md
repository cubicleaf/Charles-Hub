Israeli public trust in the Trump administration collapsed in the days following the signing of the US-Iran Memorandum of Understanding, with major Israeli media turning on a president who had until recently been more popular in Israel than anywhere else in the world.

A Channel 12 poll released Thursday captured the scale of the shift: only 11 percent of Israelis believed their country had "won" the war it had jointly launched with the United States against Iran in late February 2026, while 71 percent said they no longer trusted the Trump administration to safeguard Israeli interests in the MoU's implementation negotiations.

The backlash was expressed in language rarely directed at a US president in Israeli media. An op-ed in Israel Hayom — owned by Miriam Adelson, an influential Trump mega-donor, and widely regarded as a Netanyahu-aligned publication — titled "You could have been the greatest president of all, but you failed" accused Trump of having signed "a surrender agreement with a murderous and cruel terror regime." Senior Israeli officials were equally direct. National Security Minister Itamar Ben-Gvir posted "All of Lebanon must burn" on X; the platform subsequently restricted the post for breaching its terms of service.

The MoU's terms — negotiated without apparent Israeli participation — required the immediate conclusion of all fighting, including in Lebanon, and both parties' commitment to respecting Lebanese "territorial integrity and sovereignty," of which Israel currently occupies around a fifth. Israel has not complied with either condition.

US officials bristled at the Israeli reaction. Vance described Trump as "the only head of state in the entire world who is sympathetic to the nation of Israel at this moment in time" and warned Israeli ministers against attacking their only remaining powerful ally. Trump himself, at the G7, said Netanyahu had "got a little excited" in the attacks on Lebanon.

Israeli political analyst Ori Goldberg framed the dispute as systemic rather than episodic. "The criticism of Israel coming from US leaders hasn't arisen because they've suddenly gained any great insight, it's because the facts have become unavoidable," he said. "Everything they're saying is correct. These are truth bombs. Israel did pull them into a war and Netanyahu did manipulate Trump." He described the situation as not a spat but a "rift."

The alliance nonetheless remained formally intact, with no threats to military aid or security commitments. On Saturday, Iran reclosed the Strait of Hormuz after Israel's continued Lebanon offensive, placing the MoU's survival in direct jeopardy — and with it the US president's most prominent foreign policy achievement.
